The City‑Kingdom of Tamassos

An Iron‑Age kingdom in central Cyprus

Overview History & Timeline Copper & Economy Archaeology & Tombs Later Legacy

Overview of Tamassos

Tamassos (also spelled Tamasos) was an ancient city‑kingdom in central Cyprus, one of the ten kingdoms of the island.

It rose to prominence in the early first millennium BCE, located on the left bank of the Pediaios River, about 21 km southwest of modern Nicosia, in the region of the villages Politiko, Episkopeio, and Perera.

History & Timeline

  • Early settlement (Chalcolithic–Late Bronze Age): The wider Tamassos area shows evidence of habitation from the Chalcolithic and Early/Middle Bronze Age, with small farming settlements later replaced by a more organized city‑kingdom.
  • Iron Age rise (c. 1050–7th century BCE): Tamassos evolved into a notable city‑kingdom, famed for its copper resources and workshops.
  • Assyrian period (c. 673 BCE): The Assyrian Prism of Esarhaddon lists Tamassos among the ten Cypriot kingdoms that paid tribute, confirming its status as an independent royal seat.
  • 4th century BCE: King Pasikypros of Tamassos sold his kingdom and royalty to Pum(i)yat(o)n, king of the Phoenician city‑kingdom of Kition, and retired to Amathous.
  • After 312 BCE: Ptolemaic rule ended the old city‑kingdom system on Cyprus, and Tamassos was absorbed into the larger imperial structure.

Copper, Trade & Economy

Copper was the lifeblood of Tamassos. The region possessed rich copper ores, and Tamassos became a major producer and exporter of copper in the Eastern Mediterranean.

Some scholars link the city to the Homeric “Temese,” described as the principal copper market of Cyprus in the Odyssey.

Because of its mines, Tamassos maintained trade agreements with powerful coastal kingdoms such as Salamis and Kition, which likely competed for influence over its resources.

Archaeology & Royal Tombs

The modern archaeological site of Tamassos has revealed several important features:

  • The temple of Aphrodite, testifying to the city’s religious life and the prosperity of the cult of the goddess.
  • Several sanctuaries, including a possible temple of Apollo and a sanctuary of the Mother Goddess (Kiveli / Cybele‑type deities).
  • A royal necropoliswith two large Archaic‑period royal tombs and several smaller ones, indicating the city’s wealth and elite burial customs.
  • Recent finds include six unique, life‑size limestone sculptures—a pair of sphinxes and four recumbent lions—dating to the 6th century BCE.
“Tamassos’s rich copper deposits and metallurgical industry highlight its key role in the ancient metal‑trade networks of the Eastern Mediterranean.”

From Ancient Kingdom to Modern Site

After the copper mines were exhausted, Tamassos’s economic basis eroded and the city gradually declined. By the Early Christian period, the surrounding area was covered by small villages such as Politiko and Episkopeio, whose names preserve echoes of the old kingdom.

The name “Politiko” is thought to derive from the idea of a “polis” or brilliant ancient city, while “Episkopeio” recalls the ancient bishopric of Tamassos.

Today the site is an archaeological park; many of the most important finds from Tamassos are housed in the Cyprus Museum in Nicosia.
